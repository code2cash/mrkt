Anchor Effect ‚öì

Introduce your premium package first to make the standard lower-priced options seem more attractive.

Restaurants who know this principle list the priciest dish first to make other menu items seem more reasonably priced.

The same can be applied for time, quantity, size, speed, etc.

Marketing Style: AIDA

Use the Anchor Effect to grab Attention with a high-end offer, generate Interest in your range of products, create Desire for the "value" options, and prompt Action towards a purchase.

Bizarreness Effect üé≠

Your product might use unique, quirky animations for routine tasks, making them more memorable.

Add unconventional sounds for app notifications, use humor in error messages (rather than technical jargon), implement themed interfaces for holidays.

This leverages the tendency to better remember things that are out of the ordinary.

Marketing Style: 4 U's

Craft Unique content that stands out from competitors. Make it Ultra-specific with quirky details that highlight your product's distinctive features.

Confirmation Bias üîç

Align the displayed content with users' beliefs and preferences, reinforcing their viewpoints.

Highlight popular choices, showcase positive reviews, and customize recommendations based on previous behaviors and interests.

Social media creates "filter bubbles" by showing users more of what aligns with their beliefs, whether that's true or not.

Marketing Style: FAB (Features, Advantages, Benefits)

Present Features that align with users' existing beliefs, highlight the Advantages of this alignment, and emphasize the Benefits of using a product that confirms their worldview.

Decoy Effect üé£

Adding a less attractive decoy influences choices between two superior options.

The Economist subscriptions:
- Online: $59.00
- Print: $125.00
- Print + Online: $125.00

The online subscription is the cheapest, but the print-only decoy pushes customers toward the combo for better perceived value.

Marketing Style: PPPP (Picture, Promise, Prove, Push)

Picture the value of different options, Promise enhanced value with the preferred option, Prove it with price comparisons, and Push customers towards the intended choice.

Endowment Effect üíé

People tend to value items more once they own them.

Offer free trials or samples to let users feel ownership before purchase.

Allow customization of products or profiles to increase perceived value and attachment.

Marketing Style: BAB (Before-After-Bridge)

Show the Before (without ownership), the After (with the product and increased value perception), and Bridge the gap by offering trials or customization options.

FOMO (Fear of Missing Out) üò∞

Create a sense of urgency or exclusivity in your offerings.

Use limited-time deals, countdown timers, or "only X left in stock" messages.

Highlight what users might miss if they don't act quickly or join your service.

Marketing Style: PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve)

Present the Problem of missing out, Agitate by emphasizing what others are gaining, and Solve it by offering immediate action options.

Gamification üéÆ

Incorporate game-like elements to increase engagement and motivation.

Use points, badges, leaderboards, or progress bars to make tasks more enjoyable.

Create challenges or quests to encourage continued use of your product.

Marketing Style: QUEST

Qualify users for different game levels, help them Understand the rules, Educate them about rewards, Stimulate engagement through challenges, and Transition them to higher levels of product usage.

Hyperbolic Discounting üìâ

People prefer immediate, smaller rewards.

Offer instant gratification features alongside long-term benefits.

Provide quick wins or early access to certain features to hook users.

Marketing Style: 4 Cs (Clear, Concise, Compelling, Credible)

Clearly communicate immediate benefits, be Concise about instant rewards, make them Compelling, and remain Credible by not overshadowing long-term value.

Illusory Superiority ü¶ö

People overestimate their abilities compared to others.

Design interfaces that make users feel skilled or knowledgeable.

Offer "expert" options or advanced features to appeal to this bias.

Marketing Style: FAB (Features, Advantages, Benefits)

Highlight Features that cater to "advanced" users, emphasize the Advantages of using these features, and showcase the Benefits of being part of an "elite" user group.

Jamming Effect üö´

Excessive choices overwhelm and reduce satisfaction.

Streamline options to prevent decision paralysis.

Use progressive disclosure to reveal additional choices gradually.

Marketing Style: KISS (Keep It Simple, Stupid)

While not in our original list, the KISS principle applies perfectly here. Present choices clearly and simply, avoiding overwhelming users with too many options at once.
